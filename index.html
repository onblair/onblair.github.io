Author Controlled. Peer Driven.

Our mission is to reduce the inefficiencies and biases that exist in the current science publication system. Empeerial combines an open access publisher with a social network, providing universal freedom to disseminate research that anyone can discover, access, and discuss.

Meet Empeerial

A new system for publishing biomedical research. Empeerial improves the quality and quantity of biomedical literature, making the largest amount of research accessible to the widest user base, and enhances the translation of research into real-world applications.

WHAT PEOPLE ARE SAYING

“Scientific publishing is unbelievable and ridiculous… that science information should become available to people who want to know it with a year delay… peer review has become mortally sick, and the reason for the sickness is secrecy…it just begs for misuse.”

Vitek Tracz

Founder of scientific journals BioMed Central, F1000, and Current Opinions

“The case against science is straightforward: much of the scientific literature, perhaps half, may simply be untrue. Afflicted by studies with small sample sizes, tiny effects, invalid exploratory analyses, and flagrant conflicts of interest, together with an obsession for pursuing fashionable trends of dubious importance, science has taken a turn towards darkness.”

Dr. Richard Horton

Current Editor-in-Chief at The Lancet

“Everyone has the right to freely share in scientific advancement and its benefits.”

United Nations Charter

“Social media is an important tool for quickly connecting with other researchers… we must rely on networking and collaborations to build our research programs and to remain influential in our fields in order to advance scientific knowledge”

Dr. Craig McClain

Assistant Director of Science for the National Evolutionary Synthesis Center at Duke University

“The days of scientists communicating only with each other, in the languages of our individual disciplines, and relying on science journalists to translate for the public, are rapidly coming to an end… If we are putting our time and resources into communicating science but we're not on social media, we're like a tree falling in an empty forest.”

Dr. Christie Wilcox

University of Hawaii at Manoa

“A painstaking yearslong effort to reproduce 100 studies published in three leading psychology journals has found that more than half of the findings did not hold up when retested… the evidence for most published findings was not nearly as strong as originally claimed (where 97% of the original studies demonstrated statistically significant effects)…

Scientists have pointed to a hypercompetitive culture across science that favors novel, sexy results and provides little incentive for researchers to replicate the findings of others, or for journals to publish studies that fail to find a splashy result.”

The New York Times

August 27, 2015

“In the current scientific publishing system, journals are more likely to publish novel, positive results—which subtly incentivizes researchers to conduct studies that will produce exciting outcomes. In an ideal world, those incentives would disappear. Scientists would conduct experiments with an emphasis on methodological and statistical rigor, including frequent replication of studies to monitor their reproducibility…

If a scientist is judged by the quality of his research and not the results of that research, the final moment of publishing a paper becomes less important. ‘The framework becomes an environment for doing science, and the momentous occasion of publishing a paper becomes a decision of the original researcher'.”

Brian Nosek

Director of the Center for Open Science

“A search tool should be developed which would allow search across the 4000 institutional repositories and harvesting of materials irrespective of their local architecture. The system would progressively harvest more repositories over time, resulting in a faster search as the system matures, and use open standards to increase transparency.”

2015 Future of Scholarly Scientific Communication Conference

“The current system slows science by slowing communication of work, slows it by limiting the number of people who can access information and quashes the ability to do the kind of data analysis… The real people to blame are the leaders of the scientific community — Nobel scientists, heads of institutions, the presidents of universities — who are in a position to change things but have never faced up to this problem in part because they are beneficiaries of the system… University presidents love to tout how important their scientists are because they publish in these journals.”

Dr. Michael Eisen

Professor of genetics, genomics and development at the University of California, Berkeley

“After 30 years of practicing peer review and 15 years of studying it experimentally, I’m unconvinced of its value. Its downside is much more obvious to me than its upside, and the evidence we have on peer review tends to support that jaundiced view. Yet peer review remains sacred, worshiped by scientists and central to the processes of science… Peer review will become the job of the many rather than the few, and we know that the many can solve problems better than the few… [researchers] favor moving to a system of ‘publish and let the world decide,’ preferably with systems of reputation and article metrics”

Dr. Richard Smith

Editor at The British Medical Journal

“It is simply no longer possible to believe much of the clinical research that is published, or to rely on the judgment of trusted physicians or authoritative medical guidelines. I take no pleasure in this conclusion, which I reached slowly and reluctantly over my two decades as an editor of the New England Journal of Medicine”

Dr. Marcia Angell

Editor-in-Chief at The New England Medical Journal
